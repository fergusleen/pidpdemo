PiDP-11 Front Panel Notes

The PiDP-11 reproduces the look and basic behaviour of a classic DEC
front panel.  Its switches and lamps give a visible connection to the
state of the simulated machine.

The modern PiDP-11 kit was created by Oscar Vermeulen, whose work gave
today's enthusiasts a practical and handsome way to experience a
PDP-11 style system on a desk rather than in a machine room.

The simulator beneath many modern recreations is SIMH, associated above
all with Bob Supnik and later contributors.  That partnership matters:
Supnik's emulator preserves the behaviour of the machine, while
Vermeulen's front panel recreation restores something of its physical
presence.  Together they make the experience both credible and vivid.

In ordinary Unix use, the front panel is mostly interpretive rather
than essential.  The operating system is normally controlled from the
terminal.  Still, the panel provides a valuable sense of scale and of
how closely software once sat to the machine.

That physical relationship is worth noticing.  On modern systems, the
machine itself is usually hidden inside a featureless box.  Here the
state of the computer is dramatized: addresses, data paths, run state,
and control all have lamps or switches attached to them.  Even when the
panel is symbolic rather than strictly necessary, it teaches by making
the invisible visible.

For demonstrations, it helps to point out:

- the address and data lights
- the run and halt controls
- the difference between the console panel and the Unix login session

The panel makes the system legible in a way that modern hardware
usually does not.

It also gives visitors an immediate emotional hook.  A terminal window
is familiar.  A living front panel full of lamps invites questions.
That curiosity is useful, because it opens the door to explaining what
the operating system is doing and why machines of this era still matter.
