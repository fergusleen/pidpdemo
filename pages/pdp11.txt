About the PDP-11

The PDP-11 family, introduced by Digital Equipment Corporation in
1970, was one of the most influential minicomputer lines of its era.

This installation is aimed at a PDP-11/70, the top end of the Unibus
PDP-11 range.  Introduced in 1975, the 11/70 added cache memory and
22-bit physical addressing, letting serious Unix systems stretch into
the multi-megabyte range that seemed substantial at the time.

Engineers at DEC, including C. Gordon Bell and the teams around him,
helped shape a machine that was compact, elegant, and practical.  Its
instruction set used a small number of general registers plus flexible
addressing modes, which made the system attractive to both compiler
writers and operating system programmers.

That architecture matters because it was pleasant to think with.  The
PDP-11 instruction set was not merely powerful enough; it was tidy.
Students could learn it, assembly programmers could enjoy it, and C
compilers could target it well.  Much of the reputation of early Unix
for economy and clarity is tied to how well the software matched the
hardware beneath it.

Many important Unix systems were developed on PDP-11 hardware.  The
machine's size, cost, and practical performance helped Ken Thompson
and Dennis Ritchie turn Unix into a working laboratory system rather
than a purely academic one.

The 11/70 in particular carried a sense of seriousness.  It was not a
toy and not a personal machine in the later microcomputer sense.  It
was the sort of computer an institution could rely on for multi-user
work, instrument control, shared development, and day-to-day technical
computing.

By modern standards the hardware is small.  By historical standards it
was a serious computer: strong enough for multi-user work, compilers,
networking, laboratory control, and long-lived operating system ideas.

Part of the fascination today is that the scale is now human again.
You can still understand the machine as a whole, yet it is rich enough
to show why the PDP-11 became one of the defining computers of its age.
