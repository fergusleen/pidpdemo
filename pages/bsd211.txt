About 2.11BSD

2.11BSD is a late Berkeley Software Distribution for the PDP-11.
It brought a mature Unix environment to machines with a very small
address space by modern standards.

Berkeley figures such as Bill Joy, Marshall Kirk McKusick, and others
helped define the BSD tradition, while later PDP-11 work by Steven
Schultz and contributors kept that line alive on smaller hardware.
Running such a system requires care.  Memory is tight, tools are older,
and conveniences taken for granted on newer systems may not be present.

Even so, 2.11BSD is a capable environment.  It supports multi-user
operation, development tools, networking in suitable configurations,
and a broad collection of familiar Unix utilities.

That is part of the charm of 2.11BSD.  It feels constrained, but not
crippled.  You are constantly reminded that every process, every block,
and every address space matters, yet the system still offers a broad
and coherent Unix environment.  The result is disciplined rather than
merely primitive.

For many people, 2.11BSD is also an unusually readable operating system.
The scale is small enough that the relationship between hardware,
kernel, utilities, and user habits becomes easier to see.  The machine
does not disappear beneath abstraction; it stays visible.

For a PiDP-11 installation, it offers a historically grounded and
pleasantly comprehensible Unix system.

That makes it a good demonstration platform.  Visitors can see not only
what Unix looked like, but how capable it already was when measured by
clarity, portability, and the persistence of its ideas.
